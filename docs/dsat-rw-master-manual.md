# Digital SAT Reading & Writing Master Item Generation Manual

This manual provides the definitive psychometric and structural blueprints for generating test items for the Digital SAT (DSAT) Reading & Writing section. It covers all 10 question subtypes across the four official College Board domains.

## Section 1: Domain — Craft and Structure

### 1. Words in Context (WIC)

#### A. Theoretical Constructs & Psychometric Framework

WIC items evaluate high-utility academic vocabulary (Tier 2 and Tier 3) within specific contexts. Rather than testing rote memorization, these items assess the ability to resolve semantic ambiguity, recognize figurative or metaphorical extensions, and identify structural constraints like parallelisms, contrasts, and qualifications.

#### B. Strict Design & Construction Rules

- **Passage Length:** 50–100 words consisting of 2 to 4 sentences.
- **Syntactic Density:** Must utilize subordinate clauses, passive voice, or parentheticals to separate the blank `_______` from its immediate contextual clues.
- **Context Clues:** Must contain a clear, non-redundant semantic trigger (e.g., an appositive, a restrictive relative clause, or a coordinate conjunction) that points uniquely to one meaning.

#### C. Distractor Engineering Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Collocation Decoy** | A word that frequently appears next to words in the passage but makes no logical sense in the specific sentence blank. |
| **Flat Literal** | The primary, most common definition of the word, which fails to match the highly specific figurative or secondary meaning demanded by the context. |
| **Tone Inversion** | A word with the correct semantic category but the opposite connotation or register (e.g., highly informal or overly emotional). |

#### D. Difficulty Scaling Matrix

- **Easy:** Single-sentence clue; primary definition of a common academic word.
- **Medium:** Clues spread across two sentences; moderately abstract vocabulary.
- **Hard:** Syntax deliberately separates the clue from the blank; relies on the secondary or tertiary meaning of a common word, or an advanced, abstract Tier 3 word.

### 2. Text Structure and Purpose (TSP)

#### A. Theoretical Constructs & Psychometric Framework

TSP items measure the student's ability to analyze the rhetorical architecture of a text, track transitions in perspective or narrative focus, and define the primary function of specific sentences or the text as a whole.

#### B. Strict Design & Construction Rules

- **Passage Length:** 90–140 words structured in a single cohesive paragraph.
- **Rhetorical Pivot:** Must contain at least one major logical pivot (e.g., from a historical consensus to a modern challenge, or from a concrete observation to a broader theoretical implication).
- **Option Design:** Options must be phrased using abstract, meta-cognitive verbs (e.g., delineates, reconciles, qualifies, posits).

#### C. Distractor Engineering Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Partial Scope** | Accurately describes the function of only the first or last sentence of the passage, ignoring the text's overall trajectory. |
| **Narrative Speculation** | Introduces an unsupported assumption about the author's personal motivations or future research goals. |
| **Structural Inversion** | Reverses the actual sequence of the argument (e.g., claiming the author presents a solution before defining the problem, when the reverse occurred). |

#### D. Difficulty Scaling Matrix

- **Easy:** Linear narrative with a simple, chronological transition.
- **Medium:** A straightforward argument that shifts from presenting a theory to offering a single supporting example.
- **Hard:** Multi-layered academic prose featuring a concession, a counter-perspective, and a qualified defense of a novel methodology.

---

### 3. Cross-Text Connections (CTC)

#### A. Theoretical Constructs & Psychometric Framework

CTC items assess a student's ability to synthesize, compare, and contrast arguments from two distinct, intellectually sophisticated passages. It tests the capacity to identify subtle nuances of disagreement or alignment on shared academic phenomena.

#### B. Strict Design & Construction Rules

- **Passage Length:** Two paired texts (Text 1 and Text 2), each 60–90 words.
- **Intertextual Relationship:** Text 2 must directly address a specific aspect of Text 1. Avoid total agreement or crude, binary disagreement; instead, focus on methodological disputes, boundary conditions, or interpretive differences.

#### C. Distractor Engineering Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Mutual Consensus Decoy** | Claims that the author of Text 2 agrees with a statement in Text 1 that Text 2 actually rejects or deeply qualifies. |
| **Scope Creep** | Attributes an extreme, generalized position to Text 1 or Text 2 that goes far beyond the text's guarded assertions. |
| **Out-of-Bound Contrast** | Contrasts the two texts on a metric or variable that neither text actually mentions. |

#### D. Difficulty Scaling Matrix

- **Easy:** Text 1 asserts X; Text 2 asserts the opposite of X.
- **Medium:** Text 1 makes a broad claim; Text 2 provides a specific case study that limits the generalizability of that claim.
- **Hard:** Text 2 agrees with the core findings of Text 1 but argues that Text 1's theoretical model relies on flawed assumptions or is only applicable under a highly specific, conditional variable.

## Section 2: Domain — Information and Ideas

### 4. Central Ideas and Details (CID)

#### A. Theoretical Constructs & Psychometric Framework

CID items evaluate the comprehension of explicit and implicit arguments in highly dense academic texts. Students must differentiate between secondary details (such as experimental controls or illustrative anecdotes) and the primary, non-negotiable thesis of the author.

#### B. Strict Design & Construction Rules

- **Passage Length:** 80–130 words.
- **Structure:** Typically starts with a broad context, shifts to a specific scholarly finding or historical record, and concludes with a highly nuanced, guarded summary of the implications of that finding.

#### C. Distractor Engineering Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Over-Extrapolation** | Takes a localized, modest claim from the text and generalizes it into an absolute universal rule. |
| **Reciprocal Distractor** | Swaps the roles of cause and effect or subject and object. |
| **Retrospective Detail** | Cites an accurate detail from the passage but presents it as the "main idea," failing to capture the synthesis of the entire text. |

#### D. Difficulty Scaling Matrix

- **Easy:** The central idea is explicitly summarized in the topic sentence.
- **Medium:** The main idea requires synthesizing two distinct details separated by descriptive filler.
- **Hard:** The text presents a highly nuanced historical or scientific argument where the author's primary position is conveyed through a series of concessions or qualifications.

### 5. Command of Evidence: Textual (COE-T)

#### A. Theoretical Constructs & Psychometric Framework

COE-T items assess a student's capacity to evaluate the logical relationship between a scientific hypothesis or academic claim and empirical evidence. It tests the core scientific literacy skill of identifying which specific hypothetical finding would validate or invalidate a claim.

#### B. Strict Design & Construction Rules

- **Passage Length:** 80–120 words detailing a researcher's investigation, their experimental methodology, and their explicit, often conditional hypothesis.
- **Hypothesis Complexity:** The hypothesis must be conditional (e.g., "A produces B, but only when condition C is met").
- **Option Design:** Every option must present a potential concrete experimental result. Only one option can satisfy all parameters of the conditional hypothesis.

#### C. Distractor Engineering Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Unconditional Support Decoy** | Presents an option that shows A produces B, but ignores or contradicts the required condition C. |
| **Irrelevant Variable** | Presents a statistically significant result that involves variables not mentioned in the researcher's hypothesis. |
| **Inverse Support** | Shows a trend that actually weakens the hypothesis, or supports the old paradigm the researcher is trying to disprove. |

---

### 6. Command of Evidence: Quantitative (COE-Q)

#### A. Theoretical Constructs & Psychometric Framework

COE-Q items test the quantitative literacy of synthesizing structured data tables or graphs with academic claims. It evaluates whether a student can locate precise coordinates, calculate simple differentials, and identify which data trends support or undermine a researcher's hypothesis.

#### B. Strict Design & Construction Rules

- **Passage Structure:** A 70–110 word text presenting a scientific or social science study, accompanied by a clean, highly structured Markdown table.
- **Data Integrity:** All numeric values in the options must be strictly derived from the table. Every single digit in the correct option must be mathematically correct.
- **Formatting:** All numbers, percentages, dates, and units must be formatted using LaTeX syntax (e.g., `$18.6\text{ hours}$`, `$45.2\%$`, `$10^\circ\text{C}$`).

#### C. Distractor Engineering Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Data Misread** | Cites numbers that are physically present in the table but associates them with the wrong row, column, or variable category. |
| **Under-Specified Support** | Cites correct data points that show a trend, but omits the key comparative variable or control group required to validate the multi-variable hypothesis. |
| **True-But-Irrelevant (TBI)** | Compares correct data points from the table, but those variables are not the ones mentioned in the hypothesis. |

---

### 7. Inferences (INF)

#### A. Theoretical Constructs & Psychometric Framework

INF items test rigorous deductive and inductive reasoning. The student must identify the logical consequence of a set of premises established in the text. Crucially, the correct answer must be the only logically necessary conclusion that requires zero speculative leaps or outside knowledge.

#### B. Strict Design & Construction Rules

- **Passage Length:** 80–130 words detailing a chain of facts or scientific premises.
- **Structure:** Avoid any concluding sentence. The text must end abruptly, leaving a logical gap that the student must fill.
- **Mathematical/Logical Form:** Often relies on conditional logic or transitive relationships.

#### C. Distractor Engineering Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Speculative Narrative** | Creates a highly plausible "real-world" extension of the story that is likely true in reality but cannot be proven mathematically from the exact facts provided in the text. |
| **Equivocation Error** | Redefines a key term in the premise to draw a broader, invalid conclusion. |
| **Reverse Conditional** | Assumes that because A → B, then B → A must also be true (affirming the consequent). |

## Section 3: Domain — Expression of Ideas

### 8. Transitions (TRA)

#### A. Theoretical Constructs & Psychometric Framework

Transitions measure the ability to recognize the precise logical relationship between two consecutive sentences or clauses. The correct choice must reinforce the rhetorical flow (e.g., contrast, cause-and-effect, addition, exemplification) without introducing logical redundancy.

#### B. Strict Design & Construction Rules

- **Passage Structure:** Two or three sentences totaling 50–80 words. The second sentence begins with a blank `_______` followed by a comma.
- **Symmetry:** The logical relationship between Sentence 1 and Sentence 2 must be single and unambiguous.
- **Categories:** Addition/Emphasis (Indeed, Furthermore); Contrast/Concession (However, Nevertheless, Still); Cause/Effect (Consequently, Therefore); Exemplification (For instance, Specifically).

#### C. Distractor Engineering Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Synonym Trap** | Two distractors from the same logical category (e.g., both "Furthermore" and "In addition"). |
| **Category Shift** | A transition that matches a surface-level word but violates the structural logic. |
| **Register Clash** | Transition words that are too informal (e.g., "On top of that," "By the way"). |

---

### 9. Rhetorical Synthesis (SYN / RS)

#### A. Theoretical Constructs & Psychometric Framework

RS items measure the ability to synthesize multiple unstructured factual notes to achieve a highly specific rhetorical goal. It tests structural agility, conciseness, and the capacity to filter out irrelevant information.

#### B. Strict Design & Construction Rules

- **Passage Structure:** 4 to 6 bullet-pointed notes detailing a single academic subject.
- **Rhetorical Goal:** The prompt must state a clear, often multi-part goal (e.g., "The student wants to contrast X with Y while emphasizing their shared Z").
- **Syntactic Constraints:** The correct option must satisfy all parameters of the goal using only the facts in the notes, and must remain grammatically standard.

#### C. Distractor Engineering Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Goal Under-Specification** | The option is factually accurate and uses the notes, but only achieves half of a two-part rhetorical goal. |
| **Truth without Goal** | A grammatically perfect, 100% factually accurate sentence that completely ignores the prompt's specified rhetorical focus. |
| **Unwarranted Synthesis** | Connects two facts from the notes using a logical relationship (e.g., cause-and-effect) that the notes themselves do not establish. |

## Section 4: Domain — Standard English Conventions

### 10. Boundaries (BOU)

#### A. Theoretical Constructs & Psychometric Framework

Boundaries items test punctuation conventions at clause and phrase boundaries. These items evaluate the structural boundaries between independent clauses, dependent clauses, non-essential modifiers, appositives, and parentheticals.

#### B. Strict Design & Construction Rules

- **Passage Structure:** A 60–90 word passage with a single blank `_______` at a critical structural boundary.
- **Punctuation Targets:** Semicolons, colons, em-dashes, commas with FANBOYS, introductory clauses, non-essential modifiers.

#### C. Distractor Engineering Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Comma Splice** | Joining two independent clauses with only a comma. |
| **Run-on Conjunction** | Joining two independent clauses with a coordinating conjunction but omitting the required comma. |
| **Fragmented Semicolon/Colon** | Placing a semicolon or colon before a dependent clause or fragment. |
| **Unmatched Parenthetical** | Mixing an em-dash with a comma or parenthesis to set off a single parenthetical element. |

---

### 11. Form, Structure, and Sense (FSS)

#### A. Theoretical Constructs & Psychometric Framework

FSS items test core grammatical agreement structures. This includes subject-verb agreement (especially with inverted syntax or intervening prepositional phrases), pronoun-antecedent agreement, verb tense consistency, parallel structure, and modifier placement.

#### B. Strict Design & Construction Rules

- **Passage Structure:** A 60–90 word passage containing a blank `_______` requiring a specific verb form, pronoun, or modifier.
- **Grammatical Obstacles:** Must use intervening phrases to separate the true subject from the verb, testing the student's ability to locate the true grammatical head.

#### C. Distractor Engineering Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Proximity Decoy** | A verb form that agrees with the noun closest to the blank, but disagrees with the true grammatical subject. |
| **Tense Shift** | A verb form that has the correct number but breaks the established temporal timeline of the passage. |
| **Dangling Modifier** | A modifying phrase at the beginning of the sentence that grammatically modifies the wrong subject. |

## Section 5: The Master DSAT Item-Generator System Prompt

See `scripts/lib/dsat-rw-master-prompt.mjs` for the exportable `DSAT_RW_MASTER_SYSTEM_PROMPT` and per-subtype rule objects used by generators and validators.

### Critical Universal Protocols

1. **Passage word count:** Subtype-specific limits (see `SUBTYPE_PASSAGE_WORD_TARGETS` in the prompt module).
2. **Syntax:** Scholarly register, varied sentence structures, academic tone.
3. **Mathematical notation:** LaTeX-style inline (`$15\%$`, `$10^\circ\text{C}$`).
4. **MCQ format:** Exactly four choices (A–D) with exactly one correct answer.
5. **Explanations:** Comprehensive distractor analysis with named formula tags for each incorrect option.

### Output Format

```
**Domain & Subcategory:** [Insert Domain & Subcategory]
**Target Difficulty:** [Easy / Medium / Hard]
**Passage / Table / Notes:** [Insert Passage, Table, or Bulleted Notes]
**Question Stem:** [Insert Question Stem]
**Answer Choices:** A) … B) … C) … D) …
**Correct Answer Key:** [Insert Key]
**Detailed Explanation & Distractor Analysis:**
- **Key [X] Explanation:** …
- **Distractor [Y] Analysis:** …
```
